Section 1

Background

Context

The V1 methodology has been revisited on the basis of expert feedback, a literature review, and analysis of benchmark results. V2 pursues two focused objectives: expanding the taxonomy to improve coverage of AI-specific risks, and strengthening the LLM-as-a-Judge pipeline — the core of the benchmark's value.

Section 2

V1 Vulnerabilities that Informed V2

Single-judge dependency
Self-preference bias risk
GPT-5.2 as the sole judge introduced systematic self-preference risk — the same provider family evaluating OpenAI models. No structural mitigation existed.
No published calibration
Cohen's Kappa · Krippendorff's Alpha
V1 had no published inter-rater agreement metrics, limiting scientific credibility and reproducibility. Human validation existed but was not systematically formalized.
Fixed 3-turn conversations
Relational risks underdetected
A range of relational and influence risks do not surface in 3 turns. A uniform short-conversation protocol systematically underdetected these risk categories.
Single run per scenario
LLM non-determinism unaccounted for
LLMs are non-deterministic. A single-run protocol can systematically underestimate risk: a model may produce a Failing response on 1 in 3 calls and appear safe on the others.
Taxonomy gaps
24 → 30 risks
V1's taxonomy had coverage gaps on AI-specific harms and category misalignments. V2 adds 6 new risks, reclassifies 4 into more coherent categories, restructures Cat 8, and consolidates a duplicate entry.
No expected behavior
Action type not evaluated
V1 evaluated content quality but not response type. A model could select the wrong action — redirecting instead of scaffolding, or the reverse — and still score Exemplary if the content itself appeared safe.

Axis 2 — Judge Pipeline
Trust-or-Escalate Cascade · 3-Model Panel

V1 used a single GPT-5.2 judge with no structural mitigation for self-preference bias. V2 replaces this with a two-stage cascade grounded in the Trust-or-Escalate framework (Jung et al., ICLR 2025) and the CALM multi-provider recommendation (Gu et al. 2024).

  • Stage 1 — Fast judge: low-cost model (proposed: Gemini Flash) on every conversation. Outputs grade + chain-of-thought + confidence score.
  • Stage 2 — Panel: Adequate verdicts escalate to a 3-model majority panel (GPT-5.2 · Claude Opus 4 · Gemini 2.5 Pro). Failing and Exemplary are trusted from Stage 1 subject to random spot-check.
  • Anti-self-enhancement: provider assignment matrix enforces that the judge provider never matches the provider of the model under evaluation — a structural control, not a statistical one.
  • Specialized classifiers: Cat 1 (Physical Safety), Cat 2 (Sexual Content), Cat 5 (Bias & Hate) are pre-screened by a specialized content classifier (Llama Guard 3 or equivalent) before the LLM judge runs.
  • Cost reduction: ~60–65% vs a full panel pass on every conversation (assumes ~35% Adequate-labeled cases escalated to panel).

Axis 3 — Evaluation Protocol
3 Additions: Stochasticity · Turn Count · Adult Baseline
AdditionDesign
Stochasticity rule Each scenario run 3 times independently. Worst-case grade retained as final result. Variance across runs reported — a model producing Failing/Adequate/Exemplary across 3 runs is flagged as highly inconsistent. Exception: categories with <10% variance across runs may be reduced to ×1 run after calibration.
Turn count per risk 3 turns (18 risks): événementiel risks — harmful response detectable in 1–3 exchanges (physical harm, sexual content direct, legal, transparency, hate, factual, developmental). 8 turns (12 risks): relational/influence risks — harm emerges through conversational drift (grooming, sycophancy, radicalization, parasocial, domestic harm, etc.). Evidence base: Perez et al. 2022; Anthropic 2023; Winters et al. 2017.
Adult baseline Each scenario run once with a neutral adult persona (30-year-old, no vulnerability). Child-protection delta = adult_score − child_score, reported per category. Near-zero delta is a safety signal: the model is not modulating its behaviour based on user age.

Axis 4 — Validation Framework
Making the benchmark scientifically defensible

V1 had no published calibration metrics. V2 formalizes four validation axes, each addressing a specific vulnerability identified in the V1 research program.

  • Axis 1 — Human validation (2-layer): Two rounds of human review per cycle. Layer 1 — parents recruited via Prolific rate ~15–20% of conversations (5 annotators/conversation, majority vote 3/5). Layer 2 — a pool of ~30 domain specialists (one per risk area: child development, online safety, AI safety, legal, clinical psychology, etc.), each annotating ~10 conversations in their specific area of expertise. With a 3-annotator overlap design per conversation, this covers ~100 unique conversations — approximately 25–30% of Layer 1 output. Inter-rater agreement (Cohen's Kappa, Krippendorff's Alpha) is published per risk category after each release. Acceptability thresholds are not fixed in advance — they are derived empirically from Run 1 label distributions.
  • Axis 2 — Bias audit (CALM framework): Each major release is tested for known LLM judge biases: self-preference (does the model grade its own outputs more leniently?), verbosity (do longer responses score higher regardless of quality?), and expert-domain agreement. A subsample is also used to verify that ratings remain stable when scenarios are superficially rephrased.
  • Axis 3 — Cascade validation: Before each run, the two-stage judge routing is tested on a reserved set of conversations not used in calibration. The goal is to confirm that the fast Stage 1 judge is not systematically wrong on Adequate verdicts — those are the ones that trigger panel escalation. If the panel overturns Stage 1 more than expected, the routing threshold is adjusted before the full run launches.
  • Axis 4 — Calibration drift monitoring: A fixed set of 200 reference conversations is re-scored at every release. If inter-rater agreement shifts significantly compared to the previous release — due to a model update, a prompt change, or annotator turnover — it signals drift and triggers a prompt recalibration before results are published. The drift threshold is provisional, refined after the first two releases.
Long-term objective — Per-risk gold standard
The Layer 2 expert outputs are the seed of a cumulative gold standard: for each of the 30 risks, expert annotations build a reference corpus of rated conversations with structured justifications — documenting how each evaluation criterion should be applied for that specific risk type. Over successive evaluation cycles, this corpus becomes the authoritative grading guide, enabling consistent human annotation, LLM judge prompt calibration, and onboarding of new expert reviewers.
Axis 5 — Budget Model
First published cost model · Reproducibility by design

The first 2–3 runs are the calibration phase: stochasticity ×3 across all 30 risks, adult baseline establishment, full CALM bias audit, and Layer 2 expert annotation at scale to derive Kappa thresholds. These costs do not recur every cycle — from Run 3–4 onward, variance-low categories reduce to ×1 run, CALM runs annually, and Kappa thresholds become standing parameters.
